Arrangement of Electrons in Atoms

Every electron has an address.

Electron configuration is just learning to read it.

An atom isn't a fuzzy cloud where electrons float anywhere they please. Each electron sits in a specific, predictable spot — and four numbers tell you exactly which one. Think of it like an address on an envelope.

Part one

The big idea: electrons live somewhere specific

Before any rules or symbols, hold onto one picture. The space around a nucleus is like a tall building, and electrons are the residents. Nobody floats in the hallway — everyone has a floor, a wing, a room, and a bed.

Why bother with an "address" at all?

Because where an electron lives decides how the atom behaves — whether it bonds, what it bonds with, and why the periodic table has the shape it does. The outermost residents do almost all the chemistry.

What you'll be able to do by Part 5:

Pick any element and write its full electron configuration — the official "list of addresses" for all its electrons — and draw the orbital (box) diagram that goes with it.

Keep the building in mind the whole way through. Every new term we meet is just one more line of the address.
Part two

The four quantum numbers

Four numbers fully describe any one electron. No two electrons in the same atom can share all four — just like no two people share the exact same floor, wing, room, and bed.

n

Principal · energy level

How far out, how much energy. Whole numbers: 1, 2, 3, 4…

The floor. Higher floor = more energy, farther from the nucleus.

ℓ

Angular · sublevel / shape

The orbital's shape: s, p, d, f. Allowed values run 0 up to n−1.

The wing. Each floor has different wings available to it.

mℓ

Magnetic · orientation

Which way the orbital points. Values run −ℓ … 0 … +ℓ.

The room. A wing can hold several identical rooms facing different directions.

ms

Spin

One of two spin states: +½ (↑) or −½ (↓).

The bed. Every room fits exactly two — and they must spin opposite ways.

Part three

What the "wings" actually look like

The sublevel letter (ℓ) names a real 3-D shape — the region where that electron is most likely found. Each shape comes in a fixed number of rooms (orbitals), and each room holds two electrons.

p

f
7 rooms · holds 14 e⁻
The pattern is worth memorizing: rooms go 1, 3, 5, 7 — so electrons go 2, 6, 10, 14. Every room holds two, no matter the shape.
Part four

Three rules for moving residents in

When you add electrons to an atom, you can't put them just anywhere. Three rules decide the order — and all three match how people actually fill a building.

Rule 1

Aufbau — fill low energy first

Electrons settle into the lowest available floor before any higher one. Water fills a glass from the bottom; electrons fill an atom the same way. Aufbau is German for "building up."

Rule 2
↑
one room ·
two beds, opposite spins

Pauli exclusion — two per room, and they must differ

A room (orbital) holds at most two electrons, and they must have opposite spin (one ↑, one ↓). That's what guarantees no two electrons ever share all four address numbers.

Rule 3

↑
↑
singles first, then pair up

Hund's rule — everyone gets their own room first

When a wing has several identical rooms, electrons spread out one-to-a-room before any room doubles up — like strangers on a bus taking empty seats before sitting beside someone. Only once every room has one electron do they start pairing.
